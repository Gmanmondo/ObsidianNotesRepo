# What is a Drug?
- A natural or synthetic substance that is used to produced a ==physiological or psychological effect== in humans or higher order animals
- The nature of the drug experience can be approached from two distictly different aspects of human behaviour
	- ==Physical or Psychological==

## Psychological Dependence
- Continued use of a drug caused by ==underlying emotional needs==
	- The common denominator that characterizes all types of repeated drug use
- **Emotional factors** that play a part in drug dependence:
	- Personal characteristics of the user
	- His or her expectations about the drug experience
	- Society's attitudes and possible responses
	- Settings in which the drug is used
- The ==psychological experience== with a drug can vary in intensity and is difficult to define
	- Alcohol, heroin, and cocaine lead to more abuse
	- Marijuana and codeine have a lower potential for abuse
- **Be careful when generalizing drug abuse**
## Physical Dependence
- The physiological need for a drug that has been brought about ==by its regular use==
- Taken enough can lead to ==severe physical illness== if discontinue
- Withdrawal sickness involves:
	- ==Erratic behaviour
	- ==Vomiting
	- ==Tremors
	- ==Hallucinations
	- ==Insomnia
	- ==Sweating
- The desire to avoid withdrawal is what ultimately leads to dependence
# Drug Classifications
## Narcotics
- ==A state of sluggishness or lethargy==
- Analgesics
	- They ==relieve pain== by a depressing action on the ==central nervous system==
	- Impact blood pressure, pulse rate and breathing rate
- The regular use of a narcotic drug will invariably lead to ==physical dependence==
- The most common source these narcotic drugs is Opium (extracted from poppies)
### Examples of Narcotics
- Morphine
	- Extracted from Opium and is used to synthesize heroine
- Heroine
	- Produces a "high" that is accompanied by drowsiness and a sense of well-being that generally last for three to four hours
- Codeine
	- Also present in opium but it is usually prepared synthetically from morphine
#### Synthetic Opiates Not Derived From Opium
- OxyContin
	- Prescribed for chronic pain
	- Oxycodone is active ingredient
	- Has the same physiological effects on the body as opium narcotics
	- Not derived from Opium or Morphine
- Methadone
	- Pharmacologically related to heroin
	- Appears to eliminate the addict's desire for heroin while producing minimal side effects
## Depressants
- Substances used to ==depress the functions of the central nervous system==
- Calm irritability and anxiety and may include sleep
- Include:
	- Alcohol (Ethanol)
	- Anti anxiety drugs
	- Barbiturates
	- Model cement
	- Aerosol gas propellants (Freon)
## Stimulants
- ==Increased alertness or activity==, followed by a decrease in fatigue and loss of appetite
- Amphetamine and methamphetamine, often injected.
- Cause a rush, followed by pleasure
	- Followed by a ==period of exhaustion== and a prolonged period of ==depression==
- Includes:
	- Cocaine
		- Extracted by leaves of Erythroxylin coca
		- Increased alertness and vigor, followed by suppression of hunger, fatigue, and boredom
	- Crack
		- Cocaine mixed with baking soda and water, then heated
		- Smoked in glass pipes, stimulates brain's pleasure center

## Hallucinogens
- Substance that causes marked changes in ==normal thought process, perceptions, moods, etc.==
- Marijuana
	- Most widely used illicit drug in this category
	- Most controversial drug in this class
	- Long-term effects on health are still largely unknown
	- Psychoactive ingredient: THC (==Tetrahydrocannabinol==)
	- Concentration varies with part of the plant
### More Hallucinogens
- Psilocybin (Mushrooms)
- PCP
- DMT
- LSD (Lysergic Acid)
- Salvia
- MDMA
- Mescaline
# Drug Identification
The challenge of forensic drug ID comes in ==selecting analytical procedures== that will identify a drug

- This plan, or scheme of analysis, is divided into phases
1. ==Screening Test== Not tell you exact drug. It tells you **Category**
2. ==Confirmation Test==, Single test that specifically identifies drug
**REMEMBER:** Infinite -> A few -> one specific

## Preliminary Analysis
- Common form of prelim analysis are: ==colour tests
- ==Produces characteristic colour== when exposed to certain chemicals
- Microcrystalline tests, can also be used to identify specific drugs by studying ==size and shape of crystals==

## Conformational Determination
- Performed **after** preliminary analysis
- To specifically identify a drug, we typically use:
1. Infrared spectrophotometry
	1. ==IR -> Sample -> Info==
2. Gas Chromatography-mass-spectrometry
	1. ==Separates compounds==
	2. ==Fancy Scale (weight -> identify)==

## History of Chromatography
- Russian botanist
- Separation of plant pigments on a calcium carbonate column
	- Chlorophylls
	- Xanthophyll
- "Chroma" "graphien"

## Chromatography
- A means of ==separating and tentatively identifying== the components of a mixture
- In chromatography, one phase is always made to move in one direction over a stationary or fixed phase
- Those materials that have a preference for the moving phase will slowly pull ahead and separate from those substances that prefer to remain stationary

## Qualitative vs. Quantitative
- How we analyze drugs is dependent on the ==type of information we need==
- Qualitative info relates to the ==identity==
- Quantitative info relates to the ==amount==

# Forensic Toxicology
Drugs cause deaths related to:
- Administration of drugs (intentional or unintentional)
- Erratic/uncontrollable behaviour of individuals under the influence of drugs 

**The Role of the Forensic Toxicologist**
1. Charged with the responsibility for ***detecting*** and ***identifying*** the presence of drugs and poisons in the body fluids, tissues, and organs
2. Assessing the level of impairment associated with drug use
- work in crime labs
- may also be in hospital labs

## Toxicology of Alcohol
- A major branch of forensic toxicology deals with the measurement of alcohol in the body for violations of the law.
- Exemplifies the primary objective of forensic toxicology:
	- The **detection** and **isolation** of drugs in the body for the purpose of determining their influence on the human body
- Alcohol, or ethyl alcohol, is a colorless liquid normally diluted with water and consumed as a beverage.
- Most heavily abused legal drug
- Affects primarily central nervous system, and brain
- Blood alcohol concentration (BAC) has been shown to be directly proportional to the concentration of alcohol in the brain

## Alcohol's Fate in the Body
- All chemicals that enter the body are broken into easier to eliminate chemicals
- This process is called metabolism
- Consists of three basic steps:
### Absorption
- In blood minutes after ingestion
- Different factors affect absorption
	- Time taken to consume
	- Alcohol content
	- Amount consumed
	- Quantity and type of food in stomach
### Distribution
Through blood stream
### Elimination
Through two mechanisms:
- Oxidization
	- Liver turns alcohol into acetic acid, then into CO2 and water
- Excretion
	- 5% of alcohol is excreted in the breath, urine, and perspiration

## BAC
weight per volume
- ==0.10% -> 0.10g per 100ml blood
- "burn-off" rate of alcohol varies, but 0.015%/hr is the average

- Experimental evidence verified that the **Amount of alcohol in the breath is in direct proportion to the blood concentration and that is directly proportional to alcohol in the brain**
- The current legal measure of DD in the US and CAN is 0.08%, or 0.08g per 100ml of blood.

## Role of the Forensic Toxicologist
- Beyond the analysis of alcohol, the toxicologist is confronted with a maze of drugs and poisons
	- Present in the body
	- Requested to be tested
- Forced to use general screening procedures to narrow thousands of possibilities to one
- Assess the toxicity of the drug or poison

# Firearms, Toolmarks, and other impressions
## What is a Tool mark?
- impression can reveal class characteristics
- Presence of any **minute imperfections** on a tool imparts ==individuality==
## Individualization of Toolmarks
- Individualizations frequently becomes an attainable reality in firearm and tool mark examination.
- Structural variations and irregularities caused by ==scratches==, ==nicks==, ==breaks== and ==wear== may permit the criminalist to relate:
	- ==A scratch or abrasion to a single tool
	- ==A tire track to a particular auto
	- ==A bullet to a gun==
# Firearm Identification
- Common tasks of a forensic firearms lab:
1. Comparison of bullet casings
2. Detection and characterization of gunpowder residues
3. Estimation of muzzle-to-target distances
4. Restoration of obliterated serial numbers on weapons

# Types of Firearms
## Hand Guns
- Designed to be fired with ==one hand==
- Three most common types:
	- Single Shot
	- Revolvers
	- Semiautomatics
- Can be classified as single-action or double-action

## Long Guns
- Fired while rested on the shoulder
- Shotguns
	- Shots tiny pellets
	- generally have ==smooth barrels==
	- Shell ammunition contains numerous ball shaped projectiles called ==shot==
- Rifles
	- Feature a barrel with ==lands and grooves==
	- bullet ammo is impressed with the grooves

# Firearm Impressions
- Firearms leave many kinds of 'toolmarks'
	- Barrel of gun
	- Firing pin
	- Breech face
	- Ejector
- Often these markings are specific to a type of gun
- the bullet or casing found at a scene needs to be compared to the suspect gun so it gets test fired to retrieve a bullet

## Rifling
Defines the barrel's class characteristics
- number of lands and grooves
- Width
- Direction of twist (L or R)

### Striations
- ==Fine lines== found in the interior of the barrel due to ==imperfections== in rifling methods
- Microscopic and randomly irregular
	- unique to each barrel
	- Form the individual characteristics of the barrel

- No two barrels have identical striation markings

## Comparing Bullet Markings
- Bullet acquires features of the barrel as it is fired
	- Class characteristics - direction of twist, number of grooves
	- individual characteristics - random striations unique tot the barrel
- Examiner must obtain "test bullets" fired through the suspect barrel for comparison
- Start with class characteristics
- if they have the same class you move onto striations

# Distance Determination
Things that exit a firearm
- Bullet
- Smoke
- Unburned and partially burned gunpowder
- If the muzzle of the weapon is sufficiently close, these products will be deposited onto the target
	- These produce patterns on:
		- victim's clothing
		- Test patterns generated from suspect weapon

## Primer Residue on Hands
Gunpowder and primer residues blow back onto the shooter
- Often the firing hand
Provides info as to if they fired a weapon

- They measure the amount of **barium and antimony** on the portions of the hand
	- ==Lead
	- ==Antimony
	- ==Barium
